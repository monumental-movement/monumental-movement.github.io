### Birth of Hi-NRG and gay club culture

From the late 1970s to the 1980s, Hi-NRG (High Energy) emerged as an electronic and fast-paced dance music derived from disco.
The sound was characterized by fast tempos (120-140 BPM), synth-based riffs, and dramatic female vocals, and had a more cathartic and energetic feel than traditional disco.

Hi-NRG had a particularly strong following in gay clubs. After the Stonewall Riots in the 1970s, the LGBTQ community sought a place for their expression and identity in the club scene, and Hi-NRG functioned as their "music of liberation."

### Relationship between representative artists and LGBTQ people

**Sylvester**

A singer who was openly gay. Their signature song "You Make Me Feel (Mighty Real)" is the origin of Hi-NRG and is a timeless masterpiece as a gay anthem.

**Hazell Dean**

A UK Hi-NRG singer who was so supported in the club scene that she was called the "gay queen." Many of her songs speak for LGBTQ love and desire.

**Divine**

While active as a drag queen, she gained worldwide attention with Hi-NRG. They boldly displayed sexual expression and integrated music and gender politics.

**Evelyn Thomas**
A diva who defined the genre with her signature song "High Energy." It was explosively popular in gay clubs and spread Hi-NRG worldwide.

### Hi-NRG and the HIV/AIDS crisis

In the 1980s, HIV/AIDS struck the LGBTQ community and claimed many lives.
During this period, Hi-NRG became both an escape to forget one's sorrows and a soundtrack of life and death in memory of fallen comrades.

Sylvester himself died of AIDS, but his music continues to be played in clubs today.

<hr>

### Hi-NRG's legacy and modern impact

Hi-NRG eventually evolved into Chicago house and Eurobeat, and has left an influence on today's EDM and hyperpop.
Particularly in the LGBTQ community, Hi-NRG classics continue to be played at Pride events and gay clubs, providing a way to relive that sense of freedom.

Contemporary Drag Race culture and hyper-pop artists (SOPHIE, Kim Petras, etc.) carry Hi-NRG's DNA of exaggerated energy and gender expression.

### More than just dance music, it's a symbol of freedom and liberation

Hi-NRG is more than just "dance music"; it is a genre that embodies the history, struggle, and liberation of the LGBTQ community.
Driving beats and shimmering synths were a celebration of life and a means of escaping society's oppression for gay clubs in the 1980s.

Even today, Hi-NRG continues to be reevaluated as an essential presence when discussing the relationship between music and sexuality.
